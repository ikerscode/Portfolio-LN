Where to Look, Not Where to Shoot

Liam Nichele  ·  2026  ·  5 min read

I build software for defence, and people sometimes assume that means I build weapons. It doesn't, and the distinction is the whole point.

There's a line running through military software that doesn't get talked about enough. On one side is understanding: working out what's in a place, what's there now that wasn't yesterday, what a pile of sensor data actually means. On the other side is action: deciding what to do about it, and increasingly, deciding automatically. Most of the interesting, genuinely modernising work sits on the understanding side. Most of the risk sits on the other.

When I built VANTAGE, I wrote the boundary into the README before I wrote half the features. It reports what's present and what changed in overhead imagery, and nothing past that. No targeting, no fire-control, no strike planning, no sensor-to-shooter automation. I get why that might read as a disclaimer. It isn't. It's a design decision, and it changed what I built.

The instinct behind that boundary comes from how I actually work, not from a values statement I bolted on afterward. My strongest skill isn't any one language or framework, it's data science: taking a rough, half-formed idea nobody's built cleanly yet and turning it into a system that holds up. I like building around ideas that don't already have an off-the-shelf answer, and that habit comes with a cost, which is that I don't trust anything I build until I've tried hard to break it. Stress testing ate more hours on VANTAGE than the original build did. Feed it a cloudy scene. Feed it a shadow that looks like a hull at low tide. Turn the detection threshold down until it starts guessing at things that aren't there. You learn what a system actually does under pressure, not what it does in the demo.

That habit is exactly why the two layers can't be treated the same. When a system's job is to help someone understand a situation, you can push it until it breaks and walk away with something useful. Now you know it goes quiet on hazy imagery, or flags a shipping container as a vessel, and you design around that. A change-detection result that says something moved here, go look, is still worth having even when it's wrong sometimes, because a person checks it before anything happens. Run the same stress test on a system built to help decide a strike, and every edge case you find stops being a line in a test report. It becomes a way people could die. The false positive that costs an analyst two minutes walking over to check a screen costs something else entirely three steps downstream. Same kind of bug. Nowhere near the same weight.

The hardest question isn't technical. It's where the human stays in the loop.

I'm not squeamish about defence. I think a serious, capable, modern military is something my continent needs and mostly doesn't have in software terms. A lot of what runs today is slow, old, and years behind the tools it should be, and I want to spend my career changing that. That includes hard-edged work. What I won't do is pretend the hardest question here is a technical one. It's where the human stays in the loop, and I think the answer is simple: on any decision that ends a life.

That's not a fashionable position in a field that keeps using the word "autonomy" like it's obviously good. Autonomy is great for a lot of things. It's great for pulling imagery, tiling it, running detection, watching an area so a person doesn't have to sit and stare at a screen. It is not something I want anywhere near the trigger, and "the enemy will do it, so we have to" isn't a good enough reason to hand that decision to a model I've stress tested and still can't fully trust.

So the software I want to build is the understanding layer: the part that takes a flood of raw signal and hands a person something clear enough to act on, fast enough to matter, and honest about its own confidence rather than pretending it doesn't have any. That's the layer I want to spend a career on. It's bigger than most people think, and most of what already exists is worse than it should be. The targeting layer can wait for a harder conversation than a portfolio.
